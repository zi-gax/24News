Solar storm could bring auroras farther south over the US than they typically appear

Summary

A strong G3-level geomagnetic storm, triggered by recent coronal mass ejections from the sun, is expected to produce spectacular northern lights visible across parts of the U.S. this week. The auroras may be seen as far south as New York and Pennsylvania, with the best viewing opportunities in Alaska, the Pacific Northwest, and the Midwest. This increased auroral activity is due to the sun reaching its solar maximum, the peak of its 11-year cycle. While offering stunning visuals, such solar storms can also disrupt radio, GPS, and power systems. For optimal viewing, experts recommend finding dark, north-facing locations away from city lights between 10 p.m. and 2 a.m.​

Highlights

  • G3-level solar storm is causing spectacular northern lights, visible unusually far south, even in parts of New York and Pennsylvania.
  • The event is linked to the sun’s solar maximum, increasing geomagnetic activity and auroral displays.
  • While beautiful, the storm may disrupt GPS, radio signals, and power systems — caution is advised.
